Mapping Religion

Information designer Richard Vijgen and documentary filmmaker Bregtje van der Haak exhibited a nice object at this year's IDFA: the Atlas of Pentecostalism is a dynamic database about pentecostalism, the fastest spreading religion on this planet. The project lies at the intersection of research, journalism, data collection, visualization and art; it is that kind of multifaceted objects that people engaging in Cultural Analysis usually search for. Learning to code can help me understand the technological side of such projects, and put questions of data analysis in relation to topics like (in this case) precarity, religion and coloniality.

Virality/Contagion

I recently wrote a paper of notions of immunity and contagion in contemporary US american science fiction (related to racism, migration and coloniality). During the research, I also stumbled over this app called plague, which will basically 'infect' your phone with information and spread it. This is about 'going viral' in the most literal sense of the word, and can help us pose interesting questions about notions of virality, contagion, travelling information and more. It would be an amazing start for a research to follow certain types of information through the web; ; which type of information is 'contagious' enough to travel far? Where does it go?

Data Heart

About a year ago, artist Jen Lowe started to post her heartbeat online. It is visualized in different colors; you can see her pregnancy, for example, through change of color to a lighter red. The project is a nice example for beautiful data visualization on the one, and a questioning of gender, data collecting and privacy on the other hand.I started looking into her code to learn something, but haven't come very far until now. Her project as such, though, will be part of a paper I am writing about precarity and pregnancy.
